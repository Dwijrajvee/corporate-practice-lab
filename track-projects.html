Module 1: Understanding the Power Sector Framework

1. The Foundation: Electricity Act, 2003

Before you draft any contract in this sector, you must understand that electricity is a regulated sector. You cannot just buy and sell power at any price.

ðŸ’¡ Practical Insight In a law firm, you will mostly deal with Generation (Power Plants) and Distribution (Discoms). Transmission is mostly government-controlled (PGCIL).

The most important concept for a junior lawyer is Tariff Determination (How price is decided).

Section 63 (Bidding Route): Tariff is discovered through a transparent bidding process (Reverse Auction). If the process is followed, the Regulator (CERC/SERC) must adopt this tariff.

Most modern solar/wind projects use Section 63. This means contracts are standard and hard to negotiate.

2. The Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)

A PPA is a long-term contract (usually 25 years) between a Generator (Solar Park) and a Buyer (Discom/SECI).

The Golden Rule of PPAs: "Cash flow is King."

Banks lend money to build the plant based solely on the PPA. If the PPA is weak, the bank won't lend. Your job as a lawyer is to protect this cash flow.

  • Must-Have Clause: Payment Security Mechanism (Letter of Credit).
  • Must-Have Clause: Change in Law (If tax goes up, tariff must go up).

3. Your Task: "Change in Law"

Scenario: You are acting for a Solar Developer. They signed a PPA in Jan 2024.

In Feb 2024, the Govt increased the Basic Customs Duty (BCD) on solar modules by 20%. This increases your project cost significantly.

Objective: You need to draft a notice to the Discom claiming compensation under the "Change in Law" clause of the PPA.
